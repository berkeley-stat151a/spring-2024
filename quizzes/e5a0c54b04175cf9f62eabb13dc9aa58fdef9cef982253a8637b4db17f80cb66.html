1 Question 1 \(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

For this question, we’ll consider the linear model \(y_n = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \w_n + \beta_2 \z_n + \res_n\).

(1a)

Write the set of equations

\[ y_n = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \w_n + \beta_2 \z_n + \res_n \quad\textrm{for }n \in \{1, \ldots, N\} \]

in matrix form. That is, let \(\X\) denote an \(N \times 3\) matrix, \(\Y\) and \(\resv\) length–\(N\) column vectors, and \(\betav= (\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2)^\trans\) a length–\(3\) column vector. Then express the matrices \(\Y\), \(\X\), and \(\resv\) in terms of the scalars \(\y_n\), \(\w_n\), \(\z_n\), and \(\res_n\) so that \(\Y= \X\betav+ \resv\) is equivalent to the set of regression equations.

(1b)

Define the following quantities: \[ \begin{aligned} \overline{y} :=& \meann \y_n & \overline{z} :=& \meann \z_n & \overline{w} :=& \meann \w_n &\\ \overline{zz} :=& \meann \z_n^2 & \overline{ww} :=& \meann \w_n^2 & \overline{zw} :=& \meann \z_n \w_n & \overline{zy} :=& \meann \z_n \y_n \\ \end{aligned} \]

Write an explict expressions for \(\frac{1}{N} \X^\trans\X\) and \(\frac{1}{N} \X^\trans\Y\) in terms of these quantities.

(1c)

For this part of the question only, assume that

\[\overline{zw} = \overline{z} = \overline{w} = 0\].

In terms of the quantites defined in part (b), write a closed–form expression for \(\betavhat\), the OLS estimator of the vector \(\betav\).

Hint: The inverse of a \(3\times 3\) diagonal matrix is given by

\[ \begin{pmatrix} v_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & v_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & v_3 \\ \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1^{-1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & v_2^{-1} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & v_3^{-1} \\ \end{pmatrix} \]

2 Question 2 \(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

For this question, consider the linear models

\[ \begin{aligned} y_n ={} \betav^\trans \xv_n + \res_n &\quad\textrm{and}\quad y_n ={} \gammav^\trans \zv_n + \eta_n \end{aligned} \]

with

\[ \begin{aligned} \xv_n ={} (1, \x_n)^\trans &\quad\textrm{and}\quad \zv_n ={} (1, \z_n)^\trans \textrm{ where } \z_n :={} 10 \x_n \end{aligned} \]

Assume that \(\x_n\) is not a constant (i.e., for at least one pair \(n\) and \(m\), \(\x_n \ne \x_m\).).

Let \(\X\) denote the \(N \times 2\) matrix whose \(n\)–th row is \(\xv_n^\trans\), and \(\Z\) denote the \(N \times 2\) matrix whose \(n\)–th row is \(\zv_n^\trans\).

(2a)

Using the definitions above, find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(\A\) such that \(\zv_n = \A \xv_n\). Then, show that \(\Z = \X \A^\trans\) for the same matrix \(\A\).

(2b)

Suppose you know that the OLS estimate of \(\betav\) is given by \(\betavhat = (4, 30)\). What is the value of \(\gammavhat\), the OLS estimate of \(\gammav\)? Please use the definitions above and your answer for part (a), and justify your answer.

(2c)

Now consider the two models’ prediction on a new datapoint with \(\xv_\new = (1, 50)^\trans\) — and so, necessarily, \(\zv_\new = (1, 500)^\trans\), with respective prediction errors

\[ \res_\new^\beta := \y_\new - \betavhat^\trans \xv_\new \quad\textrm{and}\quad \res_\new^\gamma := \y_\new - \gammavhat^\trans \zv_\new. \]

Please select which of (a), (b), (c), or (d) is correct for this particular value of \(\xv_\new\) and \(\zv_\new\):

  1. It is always the case that \(\abs{\res_\new^\beta} = \abs{\res_\new^\gamma}\)
  2. It is always the case that \(\abs{\res_\new^\beta} < \abs{\res_\new^\gamma}\)
  3. It is always the case that \(\abs{\res_\new^\beta} > \abs{\res_\new^\gamma}\)
  4. In general, we cannot determine the relationship between \(\abs{\res_\new^\beta}\) and \(\abs{\res_\new^\gamma}\) using the information provided.

Briefly justify your answer.

3 Question 3\(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

For this question, assume that you have access to a function

\[ \Phi(z) = \prob{\RV{\z} \le z}, \]

as well as its inverse,

\[ \Phi^{-1}(p) = z \textrm{ such that }\prob{\RV{\z} \le z} = p, \textrm{ for }p \in [0,1], \]

where \(\RV{\z} \sim \gauss{0,1}\) denote a scalar-valued standard normal random variable.

(3a)

Suppose that \(\RV{\y} \sim \gauss{\mu, \sigma^2}\), where \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are known. Using only the functions \(\Phi(\cdot)\), \(\Phi^{-1}(\cdot)\), and the known quantities \(\mu\), and \(\sigma\), find find a quantity \(\a\) such that

\[ \prob{\RV{\y} \le \a} = 0.90. \]

Note that \(\Phi(\cdot)\) is only for a standard normal random variable. Please do not assume that you have direct access to the distribution and quantile functions of generic normal random variables.

Please justify your answer carefully.

(3b)

Now, consider the OLS estimator under normal assumptions, so that

\[ \betavhat \sim \gauss{\betav, \sigma^2 (\X^\trans\X)^{-1}}. \]

Note that \(\betavhat\) and \(\betav\) are \(P\)–dimensional vectors, \(\sigma\) is a scalar, and \((\X^\trans\X)^{-1}\) is a \(P\times P\) matrix.

Assume that \(\betav\), \(\sigma\), and \(\X\) are all known. In terms of these quantities, find the distribution of \(\betavhat_1\), the first component of \(\betavhat\).

(3c)

Combining your answers from parts (a) and (b), find a scalar \(b\) such that

\[ \prob{\betavhat_1 \le b} = 0.90. \]

4 Question 4\(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

For this question:

  • Let \(\xv_n = (1, \z_{n})^\trans\), where \(\z_n \sim \gauss{0,1}\).
  • Assume that \(\y_n = \betav^\trans \xv_n + \res_n\) for some \(\betav\) and each \(n\).
  • Assume the residuals \(\res_n\) are IID with \(\expect{\res_n} = 0\) and \(\expect{\res_n^2} = 1\), but not necessarily normal.
  • Assume that the residuals \(\res_n\) are all independent of all the \(\z_n\).

(4a)

Let \(\X\) denote the \(N\times P\) matrix consisting of the observation \(\xv_n^\trans\) in the \(n\)–th row, and let \(\Y\) denote the \(N\)–vector with \(\y_n\) in the \(n\)–th entry.

Write the matrices \(\frac{1}{N} \X^\trans \X\) and \(\frac{1}{N} \X^\trans \Y\) in terms of \(\beta\), \(\res_n\), \(\z_n\), \(N\), and constants. Note that \(\X^\trans \X\) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix and \(\X^\trans \Y\) is a \(2\)–vector.

(4b)

Evaluate the following limits:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \X^\trans \X \rightarrow ? \quad\quad\textrm{and}\quad\quad \frac{1}{N} \X^\trans \Y \rightarrow ? \\ \]

Note that your answers may depend on \(\beta\), but should not depend on \(\xv_n\) or \(\y_n\), since they are limiting quantites that do not depend on the particular dataset.

Justify your conclusion carefully (state which theorems you use).

(4c)

Using your answers from part (a) and (b), find the limit of

\[ \betavhat = \left(\X^\trans \X \right)^{-1} \X^\trans \Y \rightarrow ? \]

Justify your conclusion carefully (state which theorems you use).

5 Question 5\(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

Let \(\RV{\xv}_n\) denote an IID sequence of random \(2\)–dimensional vectors in \(\rdom{P}\) (not necessarily normal), with

\[ \RV{\xv_n} = \begin{pmatrix} \RV{\x}_{n1} \\ \RV{\x}_{n2} \end{pmatrix} \quad\quad\textrm{and}\quad\quad \expect{\RV{\xv}_n} = \zerov \quad\quad\textrm{and}\quad\quad \cov{\RV{\xv}_n} =: \Sigmam = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}. \]

(5a)

Find the limiting distribution of the vector

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sumn \RV{\xv_n} \rightarrow ? \end{aligned} \]

Justify your conclusion carefully.

(5b)

Using the univariate central limit theorem, find the limiting distributions of the difference between the components of \(\RV{\xv_n}\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sumn \left(\RV{\x}_{n1} - \RV{\x}_{n2}\right) \rightarrow ? \end{aligned} \]

Justify your conclusion carefully.

(5c)

Find a vector \(\vv\) such that \(\vv^\trans \RV{\xv_n} = \RV{\xv_n}_1 - \RV{\xv_n}_2\). Using this vector, show that the solution to (b) also follows from the solution to (a) and the continuous mapping theorem.

Justify your conclusion carefully.

6 Question 6\(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

Given a regression on \(\X\) with \(P\) regressors, and the corresponding \(\Y\), \(\Yhat\), and \(\reshat\), define the following quantities: \[ \begin{aligned} RSS :={}& \resvhat^\trans \resvhat & \textrm{(Residual sum of squares)}\\ TSS :={}& \Y^\trans \Y & \textrm{(Total sum of squares)}\\ ESS :={}& \Yhat^\trans \Yhat & \textrm{(Explained sum of squares)}\\ R^2 :={}& \frac{ESS}{TSS}. \end{aligned} \]

6a

  1. Prove that \(RSS + ESS = TSS\).
  2. Express \(R^2\) in terms of \(TSS\) and \(RSS\).

6b

  1. What is \(R^2\) when we include no regressors? (\(P = 0\))
  2. What is \(R^2\) when we include \(N\) linearly independent regressors? (\(P=N\))
  3. Can \(R^2\) ever decrease when we add a regressor? If so, how?
  4. Can \(R^2\) ever stay the same when we add a regressor? If so, how?
  5. Can \(R^2\) ever increase when we add a regressor? If so, how?

6c

These questions will be about the F-test statistic for the null \(H_0: \betav = \zerov\),

\[ \phi = \betahat^\trans (\X^\trans \X) \betahat / (P \sigmahat^2) \]

  1. Write the F-test statistic \(\phi\) in terms of \(TSS\) and \(RSS\), and \(P\).
  2. Can \(\phi\) ever decrease when we add a regressor? If so, how?
  3. Can \(\phi\) ever stay the same when we add a regressor? If so, how?
  4. Can \(\phi\) ever increase when we add a regressor? If so, how?

7 Question 7\(\hspace{4em}\bigcirc \leftarrow\) ‘X’ here to grade this question.

For this question, we will take

\[ \a_n \sim \gauss{0, 1} \quad\textrm{ and }\quad b_n = \a_n^3. \]

We assume the pairs \((a_n, b_n)\) are IID, but \(a_n\) and \(b_n\) are not independent. Assume that, for some \(\beta_a\) and \(\beta_b\),

\[ \y_n = \beta_a \a_n + \beta_b b_n + \res_n, \]

where \(\res_n\) are IID with \(\expect{\res_n} = 0\) and \(\var{\res_n} < \infty\). The residuals \(\res_n\) and \(\a_n\) are all independent of one another. Note that the residuals are not necessarily normal.

(7a)

Let \(\alphahat\) denote the OLS estimator of \(\y_n \sim \alpha a_n\), that is, of \(y_n\) regressed on \(a_n\) alone. Note that the regression for \(\alphahat\) does not include a constant, and does not include \(b_n\).

Recall that

\[ \alphahat = \frac{\sumn \y_n \a_n}{\sumn \a_n^2}, \]

and find the limit

\[ \alphahat \rightarrow ? \]

as \(N\rightarrow \infty\).

The answer may depend on the unknown \(\beta_a\) and \(\beta_b\).

Hint: Standard properties of the normal gives that \(\expect{\a_n^3} = 0\) and \(\expect{\a_n^4} = 3\).

(7b)

Letting \(\yhat_\new = \alphahat \a_\new\), find

\[ \expect{\y_\new - \yhat_\new \vert \a_\new, \Y, \A}, \]

where \(\A = (\a_1, \ldots, \a_N)^\trans\) is the vector of \(\A\) observations. Note that the expectation is conditional on the training data and on the new regressor, so the only randomness is in \(\res_\new\).

The answer may depend on the unknown \(\beta_a\) and \(\beta_b\).

(7c)

Assume that \(\beta_b \ne 0\), that \(N\) is very large.

  • What does your result from (a) imply about using \(\alphahat\) for inference on \(\beta_a\)?
  • What does your result from (b) imply about using \(\alphahat\) for prediction of \(\y_\new\)?
